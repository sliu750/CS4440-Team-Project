BLS Visualization Dashboard

This site is a visualization of fatal injury rates across the US over the past several years. Each section contains an interactive graph and general analysis of the results found after collecting this data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics. If you want to analyze the data for yourself, you can view the raw data here.

Injuries by Industry

One of the immediately notable observations is the significant variance in fatal injury rates between industries. Agricultural, transportation, warehousing, mining, extraction, and construction industries consistently show higher fatal injury rates over the years. These "blue-collar" industries are known for their hazardous working conditions (especially compared to the lower-risk "white-collar" industries seen far below on the graph), but despite improvements in technology and greater cultural awareness of working conditions in the US, these industries continue to lead the US in fatalities on the job.

There are some visible fluctuations within these high-risk industries, which could be indicative of industry-shaping events, disasters, or regulatory policies that may have occurred within those timeframes. Investigating these fluctuations could be a potential research topic for future labor data analysts. Generally, these high-risk industries have still seen a decline over the 14 years of data. However, the gradual rate of improvement does not dismiss the abnormally high fatality rates present in these industries. Workers, industry officials, and US regulators should seek to enact & enforce regulations and standards so that these industries are more comparable to their white-collar counterparts.

Injuries by Occupation

Roofing, logging, fishing, trapping, and hunting jobs have had significantly higher fatal injury rates compared to other occupations. This is despite a sharp decline in fatal injury rates for the logging industry, and is even significantly higher than other traditional blue-collar occupations like transportation or electricians. This suggests future research into why the aforementioned occupations are so notoriously dangerous even amongst other blue-collar work.

Injuries by Demographic

Workers aged 65 and older have a significantly higher fatal injury rate than other ages. In fact, fatal injury rates generally tend to correlate higher with age regardless of year. This could provide some economic rationale as to why some employers may engage in age discrimination against hiring older employees due to the higher risk of injury - this issue should be explored in future research.

With respect to gender, men have a significantly higher fatal injury rate than women (who had the lowest fatal injury rate). Many of the high-risk industries and occupations graphed in previous sections are culturally known as male-dominated fields, which could be a potential (albeit unexplored) explanation as to why men have a higher fatal injury rate than women. Most races had similar fatal injury rates with the exception of Asians, who had the second-lowest fatal injury rate by demographic (women having the lowest). This phenomenon could have many explanations, none of which immediately stand out as a potential justification. Thus, more research should be done as to what occupations, industries, and other contributing factors have led to such an abnormally low fatal injury rate.
